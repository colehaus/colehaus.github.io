Strategic voting in the moral parliament

Objecting to a claim in MacAskill's Normative Uncertainty

Published on April 17, 2018.

Moral uncertainty

Briefly, moral uncertainty in (MacAskill 2014) starts from the premise that we will never have absolute certainty as to the one, true moral theory (Just as we don’t in the emprical realm. See, for example, fallibilism.). How should we account for this? Do we just ignore all theories other than the one which we grant the most credence? MacAskill proposes that we don’t and instead combine the results of different theories using the tools of social choice theory. In particular, he suggests that we allow moral theories to cast weighted “votes” and that we reach a final decision by using the Borda count on these votes.

(If this explanation is too rushed, chapter 2 of (MacAskill 2014) explains it all at greater length. It’s quite readable.)

We can’t have nice things

If you have a social choice sense, it should be tingling now. Aren’t there a bunch of impossibility results in social choice theory saying, roughly, that no voting system is satisfactory? Alas, yes. See, for example, Arrow’s impossibility theorem and the Gibbard-Satterthwaite theorem. These seem like shaky grounds to build the core of your moral metatheory…

What about honest people? Can honest people have nice things?

MacAskill claims to sidestep these results because tactical voting is irrelevant in the moral parliament. Moral theories are not agents and cannot dissemble:

It should be clear that this [strategic voting] consideration should bear no weight in the context of decision-making under moral uncertainty. We have no need to worry about theories “lying” about their choice-worthiness function (whatever that would mean). The decision-maker knows what moral theories she has credence in, and she knows their choice-worthiness functions. So, unlike in the case of voting, there is no gap between an individual’s stated preferences and an individual’s true preferences.

Sadly, no

Social choice theory doesn’t call out strategic voting in order to condemn those tricksy voters for their lack of virtue. Strategic voting is a problem because it reduces the quality of information available for making social decisions1. The ultimate goal of any social choice mechanism is social efficiency—the social decision should always maximize aggregate welfare. Inaccurate information as to what individuals want makes that (in the general case) impossible. If somebody insists they adore Vermin Supreme despite a private loathing, no voting system2 can remedy that.

Is it possible to have inaccurate information in the moral parliament? Of course. Even without agency, there can be yawning chasms between our moral beliefs and the moral fact of the matter.
